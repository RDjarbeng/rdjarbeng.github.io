Have you ever opened your inbox to find a brief, slightly accusatory email from a complete stranger? It usually looks something like this:

> **Subject:** (No Subject) or Re: Following up
> **From:** Random Name [random.address123@gmail.com](mailto:random.address123@gmail.com)
> Please, why are you not replying to my emails?

If your first instinct is to reply and clear up the confusion, stop. You are likely being targeted by a social engineering tactic known as **curiosity bait**.

Here is a breakdown of how this scam works, what the senders are really after, and how to protect your inbox.

## How Curiosity Bait Works

![Screenshot of an email with content 'Please, why are you not replying to my emails?'](/assets/images/20260622-154114.png "Screenshot of Phishing/Spam email")

Unlike classic phishing emails that demand immediate payment or ask you to click a malicious link to "reset your password," curiosity bait plays a longer game. It relies on human nature. When accused of ignoring someone, our instinct is to respond, usually with something like, _"Who is this?"_ or _"I think you have the wrong address."_

The scammers do not actually want to have a conversation with you. They are using this tactic to achieve a single goal: **verification**.

If you reply, you confirm several valuable pieces of information:

1. Your email address is active and functioning.
2. The account is monitored by a real human, not an automated system.
3. You are willing to open, read, and engage with emails from unknown senders.

Once an email address is verified as "active and engaged," its value on the dark web or among spam networks skyrockets. Your address will be added to premium target lists, practically guaranteeing an influx of more sophisticated phishing attempts, malware links, and spam.

## How to Spot the Red Flags

These emails are intentionally designed to be vague, but they share common characteristics that give them away:

* **The False History:** They refer to previous emails, invoices, or conversations that simply do not exist.
* **Zero Context:** A legitimate person trying to reach you will provide context so you know what they are talking about (e.g., _"Did you receive the brief I sent on Tuesday?"_). Scammers leave out details so the email can be blasted to millions of people at once.
* **Mismatched Sender Details:** Often, the display name will not match the email address at all (e.g., the display name says "John Smith," but the address is `marylansky9@gmail.com`).
* **Free Email Providers:** The sender is usually using a free, disposable address from services like Gmail, Yahoo, or Outlook, which are easy for spammers to create and abandon in bulk.

## What You Should Do

When you spot a curiosity bait email, the best defense is no defense at all.

* **Do not reply.** Even a polite "wrong address" response flags your account as an active target.
* **Do not click any links or open attachments** if they happen to be included.
* **Mark it as Spam or Junk.** This trains your email provider’s filtering algorithms to catch similar messages before they ever reach your inbox.
* **Delete the email.**

The next time a stranger demands to know why you are ignoring them, the safest move is to keep doing exactly that.
