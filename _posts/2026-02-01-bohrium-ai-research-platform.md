Air quality is a multidimensional environmental metric defined by the concentration of gaseous and particulate pollutants—including fine particulate matter (PM₂.₅), coarse particulate matter (PM₁₀), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), sulfur dioxide (SO₂), ground-level ozone (O₃), carbon monoxide (CO), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs)—in the ambient atmosphere. Its deterioration triggers a cascade of biological responses in humans, primarily through oxidative stress, systemic inflammation, and autonomic nervous system dysregulation (Haddad et al., 2024)(Hodgson et al., 2023). These mechanisms underpin associations with respiratory diseases (e.g., asthma exacerbations, COPD progression), cardiovascular morbidity (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke), neurocognitive decline, and premature mortality (Lim et al., 2023)(Howlett-Downing et al., 2023)(Hodgson et al., 2023). Critically, there is no safe threshold for PM₂.₅ exposure: epidemiological evidence demonstrates that health benefits are greatest when reducing pollution in moderately polluted environments, with log-linear concentration–response functions indicating approximately a 6% increase in all-cause mortality risk per 10 μg/m³ increment in long-term PM₂.₅ exposure (Haddad et al., 2024)(Hodgson et al., 2023).

In Africa, air quality constitutes one of the most acute yet under-addressed public health emergencies on the continent. The burden is staggering: ambient [air pollution] contributes to approximately 1.1 million premature deaths annually across Africa—accounting for one-third of the continent’s total air pollution–related mortality, while household air pollution accounts for the remaining two-thirds (Matthaios et al., 2025)(Bililign et al., 2024). Sub-Saharan Africa bears the highest population growth and urbanization rates globally, creating conditions for rapid emissions escalation from aging diesel fleets, informal industrial activity, widespread biomass combustion (for cooking and heating), unregulated waste burning, and recurrent Saharan dust events (Bililign et al., 2024)(Gahungu et al., 2022)(Isaxon et al., 2022)(Abera et al., 2021). West Africa is particularly affected, with cities such as Lagos, Abuja, and Bamako reporting daily average PM₂.₅ concentrations reaching 40.856 μg/m³—more than double the World Health Organization’s (WHO) annual guideline of 5 μg/m³ (Gahungu et al., 2022). This crisis is compounded by an extreme paucity of monitoring infrastructure: fewer than 10% of WHO-recommended ground-based air quality stations exist across the continent, rendering most national air quality assessments reliant on satellite-derived reanalysis or modelled estimates rather than empirical observation .

The trajectory of air quality research published \*from\* African institutions reflects both persistent structural constraints and accelerating momentum. Historically, air pollution science in Africa lagged far behind North America, Europe, and Asia; until recently, global emission inventories developed elsewhere were routinely applied to African contexts without local validation (Isaxon et al., 2022). Bibliometric analyses confirm this gap: less than 1% of peer-reviewed air quality literature originates from African academic institutions , and studies explicitly focused on African urban air quality remain sparse despite its severity (Fuller et al., 2022)(Matandirotya, 2021). However, a marked inflection point emerged post-2015, driven by targeted international funding (e.g., UK’s AQ-Health initiative, EU’s AMMA-2050 program), regional capacity-building networks (e.g., ACTRIS-Africa, Clean Air Initiative–Africa), and growing recognition among African researchers and policymakers . A bibliometric review of South African air quality research (2010–2021) documented steady growth in output, with increasing emphasis on source apportionment, [health impact assessment], and policy evaluation (Matandirotya, 2021). Similarly, scoping reviews of West African cities reveal a nascent but expanding body of work linking industrial zones, traffic corridors, and informal settlements to localized hotspots of PM₂.₅ and NOₓ (Alvarez et al., 2020)(Abera et al., 2021). Nevertheless, fragmentation persists: data remains scattered, methodological standardization is limited, and longitudinal studies—essential for tracking trends—are rare (Okello et al., 2022)(Borge et al., 2023).

In response to infrastructural scarcity and cost barriers, African air quality research is pioneering a suite of novel, unconventional methodologies that diverge significantly from traditional regulatory-grade monitoring. Low-cost sensor (LCS) networks have become foundational, particularly in East African megacities like Nairobi and Kampala. Projects such as Measuring Air Quality for Advocacy in Africa (MA³) deployed PurpleAir-II-SD sensors across thirteen locations in Kenya and Uganda, demonstrating feasibility for longitudinal PM₂.₅ measurement despite challenges related to calibration drift and humidity interference—findings corroborated by studies examining LCS performance under varied African environmental conditions (Awokola et al., 2020)(Katushabe et al., 2021). Complementing hardware innovation, machine learning is increasingly leveraged to correct LCS biases using co-located reference instruments and environmental covariates, thereby enhancing data credibility . Satellite data fusion represents another transformative approach: deep transfer learning models now integrate Sentinel-5P TROPOMI NO₂ retrievals with Landsat-derived land-use features and meteorological variables to generate high-resolution, city-scale air quality estimates in data-poor regions—effectively turning remote sensing into a surrogate ground-monitoring network (Yadav et al., 2023). Citizen science platforms like AirQo in Uganda further democratize data collection, enabling real-time, community-sourced pollution mapping via mobile applications . Finally, emerging frontiers include drone-based vertical profiling of aerosol layers to characterize boundary-layer dynamics over complex urban topography, and AI-driven source apportionment using anonymized mobile phone mobility data to reconstruct personal exposure pathways across microenvironments—a paradigm shift from static ambient measurements to dynamic, individualized exposure modelling .

These innovations collectively signal a maturing, contextually grounded research ecosystem—one that acknowledges infrastructural limitations not as insurmountable barriers but as catalysts for methodological creativity. Their success hinges on sustained investment in local technical capacity, open-data governance frameworks, and interdisciplinary collaboration bridging atmospheric science, public health, data engineering, and urban planning. Without such integration, even the most sophisticated sensor network or AI model risks producing technically elegant but socially disconnected knowledge—whereas the ultimate objective remains clear: generating actionable intelligence to safeguard the health of Africa’s rapidly urbanizing populations.
