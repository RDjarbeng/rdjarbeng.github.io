9	## TL;DR
10	
11	Y Combinator's Request for Startups (RFS) is their public wishlist — the ideas they _actively want_ founders to pitch them. Think of it as a cheat code: you don't have to guess what YC wants to fund. They tell you outright.
12	
13	The 2025 edition is the most AI-saturated in YC history (\~70% of categories have an explicit AI component), but it's also the most _physical_ — robotics, defense, energy, construction, and biotech all have dedicated slots. The message is clear: **software is eating the world, but AI is now eating software, and the next frontier is atoms, not just bits.**
14	
15	This report breaks down every category, identifies who's already in the arena, maps the interdisciplinary sweet spots, and ranks each idea from "build an MVP this weekend" to "you'll need a PhD and a nuclear reactor."

61	## Deep Dive: Every Category Explained
62	
63	### 1. 🤖 Large Language Models (LLMs)
64	
65	**What YC wants:** Not just another ChatGPT wrapper — they want companies competing at the frontier of model training, or building the critical infrastructure layer (evaluation, fine-tuning, deployment, compression) that makes LLMs production-ready.
66	
67	**Key sub-ideas:**
68	- New base/specialized model labs (vertical-specific LLMs)
69	- LLM evaluation and benchmarking platforms
70	- Fine-tuning infrastructure and tooling
71	- Model compression (quantization, distillation, pruning)
72	- Inference optimization (lower latency, lower cost)
73	- Synthetic data generation for training
74	
75	**Why this matters:** The LLM stack is far from settled. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google dominate, but there's enormous space in the middleware — the tools that make models _usable_ for enterprises.
76	
77	---

79	### 2. 🧠 AI Agents
80	
81	**What YC wants:** Software that _does things_, not just _answers things_. Autonomous agents that complete complex, multi-step tasks with minimal human babysitting. YC sees this as _the_ defining startup opportunity of this era.
82	
83	**Key sub-ideas:**
84	- Coding agents (autonomous software development)
85	- Research agents (literature review, hypothesis generation)
86	- Customer support agents with autonomous resolution
87	- Sales and outreach agents
88	- Legal and financial document review agents
89	- Multi-agent orchestration frameworks
90	- Browser automation / "computer use" agents
91	
92	**Why this matters:** Every knowledge worker's job is about to be rebundled. The companies that build reliable agents for specific verticals will own those verticals.

97	
98	**What YC wants:** The picks and shovels of the AI gold rush. As AI workloads explode, new infrastructure categories are emerging that don't yet have dominant incumbents.
99	
100	**Key sub-ideas:**

102	- GPU cluster management and orchestration
103	- AI observability, monitoring, and cost tracking (LLMOps)
104	- Data labeling and RLHF pipelines
105	- Prompt management and versioning
106	- Distributed training infrastructure
107	- Model serving and inference platforms
108	
109	**Why this matters:** Every AI company needs this stuff, and most are duct-taping it together. Whoever provides clean, reliable infrastructure becomes a horizontal platform company.

633	## Final Thoughts: How to Actually Use This List
634	
635	### ✅ Do This:
636	1. **Pick the intersection, not the category.** The best companies combine 2-3 RFS areas (see the Interdisciplinary Sweet Spots above). This creates natural moats.
637	2. **Start with your unfair advantage.** Former nurse? Healthcare AI. Ex-military? Defense tech. Construction engineer? Building automation. Your background IS your moat.
638	3. **Build the MVP first, pitch second.** YC funds traction over ideas. An ugly prototype with 5 paying customers beats a beautiful deck with zero.
639	4. **Read the subtext.** When YC says "AI agents," they mean "replace a $60K/year employee with a $500/month subscription." Price accordingly.
640	5. **Time your application.** YC batches run twice a year (Winter and Summer). The current RFS reflects what partners _want to see in the next batch_.
641	
642	### ❌ Don't Do This:
643	1. **Don't build a "ChatGPT wrapper."** YC explicitly wants infrastructure and vertical applications, not thin layers over OpenAI's API.
644	2. **Don't ignore the physical world.** The biggest shift in this RFS vs. previous years is the emphasis on atoms (robotics, energy, construction, defense). Software-only founders should seriously consider hardware-software combinations.
645	3. **Don't confuse "hard to build" with "bad opportunity."** The hardest categories (fusion, BCIs, custom silicon) also have the biggest moats and outcomes. YC funds early.
646	4. **Don't build in a vacuum.** Every category has incumbents. Study them. Find the gap they're missing, then wedge in.
647	5. **Don't be a tourist.** YC can tell when founders picked an RFS category because it seemed trendy vs. because they genuinely care. Authenticity wins.
648	
649	### 📊 The Meta-Pattern
650	
651	If you zoom out, YC's 2025 RFS tells a single story:
652	
653	> **AI is the new electricity, and it's time to wire up every industry that hasn't been wired yet.**
654	
655	The categories that are "bits only" (agents, infrastructure, dev tools) are the near-term revenue machines. The categories that blend bits and atoms (robotics, energy, defense, healthcare) are the long-term trillion-dollar outcomes. And the moonshots (fusion, BCIs, space) are the legacy-defining bets.
656	
657	Choose the timeline that matches your ambition — and your runway.
