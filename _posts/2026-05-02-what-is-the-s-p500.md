\*\*Warren Buffett won a famous $1 million wager (which grew to about $2.22 million) that a low-cost S&P 500 index fund would outperform a selection of hedge funds over 10 years.\*\*

### Key Details
- \*\*When\*\*: The bet started on January 1, 2008, and ended on December 31, 2017.
- \*\*The Wager\*\*: Buffett bet that an S&P 500 index fund (specifically Vanguard's S&P 500 Admiral Shares, VFIAX) would beat the average performance of five funds-of-hedge-funds (selected by the other side) over the decade. Performance was measured net of all fees, costs, and expenses.
- \*\*Opponent\*\*: Protégé Partners LLC (led by Ted Seides), a firm that invests in hedge funds. They selected five undisclosed funds-of-funds.
- \*\*Stakes\*\*: Each side contributed funds (roughly $320,000 each in zero-coupon bonds) that were set to grow to $1 million by the end. The winner's share went to charity. Buffett designated Girls Inc. of Omaha; Protégé chose Friends of Absolute Return for Kids. The final pot was about $2.22 million, which went to Girls Inc. after Buffett won.

### Outcome
Buffett's S&P 500 index fund significantly outperformed. By the end of the period:
- The Vanguard S&P 500 fund returned roughly \*\*7.1% annualized\*\* (or cumulatively much higher, e.g., around 85%+ after nine years in some reports).
- The average of the five hedge funds of funds returned only about \*\*2.2% annualized\*\* (cumulatively around 22%).

The index fund pulled ahead especially after the 2008 financial crisis recovery, as high hedge fund fees and underperformance in a bull market hurt the active side.

### Context and Purpose
Buffett has long argued that most active managers (especially hedge funds with their high "2-and-20" fees or funds-of-funds with extra layers) cannot consistently beat the market after fees. This bet was a public demonstration of that view, pitting passive indexing against active hedge fund selection. He made the challenge in the mid-2000s, and Protégé accepted.

This is one of Buffett's most well-known "bets" and is often cited in discussions about passive vs. active investing. If you're looking for his annual letter comments on it or more specifics, let me know!
