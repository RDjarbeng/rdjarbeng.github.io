What happens when everyone sells shovels and no one mines?

### Why this matters today

In the 1840s, the most consistent wealth wasn't found in the gold mines, but in the pockets of those selling the picks and shovels. Today, we see a mirror image in the tech industry:

- **The "Shovels":** AI wrappers, "prompt engineering" courses, and generic SaaS templates.
- **The "Mining":** The difficult, specialized work of building or developing unique solutions that actually solve a problem.

**Additional comments:**

The classic gold rush strategy remains a potent metaphor for modern business cycles. In the 1840s, the consistent wealth was not found in the unpredictable gold mines but in the pockets of those selling the picks and shovels. We see a direct mirror of this phenomenon in the current tech landscape. The market is saturated with AI wrappers, generic SaaS templates, and endless prompt engineering courses. These represent the digital shovels. Everyone is eager to capitalize on the hype by providing tools for a gold rush that may or may not exist.

True value remains in the mining process. Mining is the difficult and specialized work of developing unique solutions that address actual problems for real users. When everyone focuses on selling the tools, the market becomes flooded with utility providers and lacks genuine innovators. Building a sustainable venture requires shifting the focus from selling the pickaxe to extracting the gold. Success belongs to those who do the hard work of identifying complex challenges and solving them with proprietary technology rather than simply selling the shovels to everyone else.
